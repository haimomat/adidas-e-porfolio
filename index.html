Section 2

Ladder of Engagement

This part is turned into a stronger visual framework section so the model is easier to understand at a glance.

02

The Ladder of Engagement shows that customers do not all engage with brands in the same way. Some users only view content, while others may comment, share, recommend or even help to generate ideas with the brand. In the workshop briefing, the Ladder of Engagement is presented as a model that explains different levels of customer engagement, ranging from lower-level actions such as ratings, reviews and discussions to higher-level participation such as collaboration and co-creation (Chaffey and Smith, 2022). The model is also different from the Ladder of Loyalty because it focuses on the depth of customer participation rather than repeat purchase alone (Smith and Zook, 2020). This is useful for marketers because it shows that digital audiences are not simply passive receivers of communication, but can also become active contributors to brand value. In practice, the higher levels of engagement are especially important because participation is usually uneven: Nielsen’s 90:9:1 rule suggests that most users remain passive, while only a small minority actively contribute content or collaborate (Nielsen, 2006).

Introducing adidas as the example brand

adidas is a suitable example of the Ladder of Engagement because the brand uses a connected digital ecosystem to encourage consumers to move beyond simple awareness into deeper forms of participation. Through adiClub, adidas offers rewards, member-only products, early access, special offers and experiences that keep users involved with the brand over time rather than only at the point of purchase (adidas, 2026a). adidas also explains that members can earn points through purchases, tracking workouts and sharing product reviews, which shows that the brand values ongoing engagement across different touchpoints (adidas, 2026a). This is supported by the company’s annual reporting, which explains that adiClub helps adidas build direct relationships with consumers across its website, apps and retail stores, while allowing members to unlock rewards and personalised experiences as they move up levels (adidas AG, 2024). As a result, adidas can be seen as a strong example of a company that engages customers at several levels of the ladder rather than simply using digital channels for one-way promotion.

Ratings, reviews and discussions

At the lower levels of the Ladder of Engagement, adidas encourages customers to take part through ratings, reviews and simple digital interactions. Although these forms of engagement seem basic, they are still important because they show that consumers are doing more than simply viewing brand content. Reviews and feedback can help other customers make decisions, while also giving the brand useful insight into customer preferences and product experience. This links closely to the lower stages of the ladder identified by Zook and Smith, where participation begins with ratings, reviews and discussions rather than full collaboration (Zook and Smith, 2016). In the case of adidas, adiClub also supports this early engagement by rewarding members for actions beyond purchase, which helps keep consumers connected to the brand across different touchpoints (adidas, 2026a). Therefore, adidas is effective at the lower levels of the ladder because it makes it easy for users to respond, interact and remain involved with the brand over time.

Discussions, ideas and deeper engagement

Beyond ratings and reviews, adidas also engages consumers at deeper levels through discussions, branded content and community-based interaction. Zook and Smith (2016) suggest that engagement can develop from simple feedback into discussions, ideas and eventually collaborative co-creation. This can be seen in the way adidas builds excitement around launches, memberships and exclusive experiences through its digital platforms, especially through CONFIRMED, which gives members access to limited releases, insider content and a more curated brand experience (adidas, 2022). This suggests that adidas is effective at moving consumers beyond passive attention towards stronger involvement with the brand. However, the highest level of engagement still appears to be limited to smaller groups of highly interested consumers rather than the whole customer base. Overall, adidas is a strong example of the Ladder of Engagement because it performs well across lower and middle levels of participation and also shows some evidence of deeper community-based engagement, even if full collaborative co-creation remains selective (Zook and Smith, 2016; Smith and Zook, 2020).

03

Introducing TikTok and how marketers use it

TikTok is a useful social media platform for marketers because it combines short-form video, personalised content discovery and business advertising tools in one space. TikTok explains that the For You feed is personalised based on users’ interests and engagement, which means brands have the opportunity to reach audiences through content that feels relevant rather than purely promotional (TikTok, n.d.b; TikTok, n.d.c). From a marketing perspective, this makes TikTok valuable for building awareness, encouraging engagement and increasing visibility through creative video content. In addition, TikTok for Business states that brands can use tools such as Ads Manager and Business Center to create, manage and optimise campaigns, showing that the platform is not only for entertainment but also for structured marketing activity (TikTok for Business, n.d.a; TikTok for Business, 2026). Therefore, TikTok can be seen as both a social media platform and an important digital marketing channel.

Critique using a textbook framework

A useful way to evaluate TikTok is through the Four Zones of Social Media framework developed by Tuten and Solomon (2017), which includes social community, social publishing, social entertainment and social commerce. First, TikTok is strong as a social community platform because users can follow accounts, comment on videos, send messages and interact with creators and brands. Second, it is highly effective for social publishing because both users and companies can create short-form videos that are easy to distribute and discover. Third, TikTok performs particularly well as a social entertainment platform, as the whole app is designed around engaging and entertaining video content. This is one of its biggest strengths for marketers because advertising content can be embedded into trends, challenges and creative storytelling rather than appearing too formal or traditional. Finally, TikTok also supports social commerce. TikTok for Business promotes tools such as TikTok Shop, while TikTok Shop guidance explains that products can be sold directly through videos, livestreams and product pages (TikTok for Business, n.d.a; TikTok Shop, 2025). This makes TikTok attractive to marketers because one platform can support awareness, engagement and sales at the same time.

Overall evaluation and limitations

However, TikTok also has some limitations for marketers. One major weakness is that success on the platform depends heavily on algorithmic visibility and the ability to produce content that feels engaging, fast-moving and native to the platform. This means that brands cannot simply repost traditional advertising and expect strong results. Instead, they need creative content that matches the style and culture of TikTok. In addition, although TikTok is strong for awareness, entertainment and increasingly commerce, it may be less suitable for brands that rely on formal or information-heavy communication. Overall, TikTok is a highly effective platform for marketers because it combines community, entertainment and commercial opportunity in one space. However, its value is greatest for brands that can adapt quickly, create visually engaging content and respond to changing platform trends (Tuten and Solomon, 2017; TikTok, n.d.c).

Trend Analysis

The final analytical section is made more dynamic with impact cards and a stronger visual feature block.

05

A digital trend that is particularly relevant to adidas is gamification in digital marketing, where brands use challenges, rewards and progress-based activities to increase user participation. This trend is especially suitable for sportswear brands because it fits naturally with ideas of performance, motivation and achievement. In digital marketing, consumers increasingly expect experiences that are interactive and enjoyable rather than only receiving one-way promotional messages. Gamification responds to this by making digital platforms more engaging and encouraging users to take part in activities linked to the brand.

Interaction

Users are encouraged to do more than browse. They participate, return regularly and engage with progress-based digital experiences.

Motivation

Challenges, rewards and activity tracking make brand interaction feel more enjoyable and personally meaningful.

Loyalty

Repeat interaction and a stronger digital experience can increase emotional connection and encourage longer-term brand attachment.

This trend reflects a shift from passive digital browsing to more active brand engagement. Rather than simply viewing content or searching for products, users are encouraged to complete challenges, track progress and return to the platform more regularly. This can make digital interaction feel more motivating and rewarding, while also increasing the time users spend with the brand. In addition, gamified features are often easier to share and repeat, which helps maintain continued participation over time.

For adidas, this trend has strong practical relevance because the brand already connects sport, lifestyle and digital platforms through its online presence and apps. Features such as running challenges, goal tracking and reward-based participation can encourage users to interact with the brand more often while also strengthening loyalty and brand experience. This is especially valuable because adidas does not only sell products, but also promotes an active lifestyle and a sense of personal achievement. Overall, gamification is a valuable digital trend for adidas because it combines interaction, motivation and brand participation in a way that fits the sportswear market well.

Overall, this report shows that adidas uses digital marketing effectively across different online touchpoints.
